# Arcanum
## Context
Welcome to Arcanum, also known as Story Engine!

## Objective
We use the game of French Tarot to drive stories, with Tarot Cards represent core narrative archetypes.
To make stories drivable, we break them down into Story Beats, which are left open to human interpretation.

## French Tarot Game
### Game Setup:
- Decide how many rounds of French Tarot are to be played, within reason. For this application, we shall play 1 - 20 total rounds.
- Decide the number of players per round of French Tarot.
- -> For three players, there are 24 tricks with 24 cards, six card dog, four card packet.
- -> For four players, there are 18 tricks with 18 cards, six card dog, three card packet.
- -> For five players, there are 15 tricks with 15 cards, three card dog, three card packet.
- Shuffle the deck, and deal each player a card.
- Tarot Suit Rankings: wands (clubs) < pentacles (diamonds) < cups (hearts) < swords (spades).
- The Major Arcana are ranked in their given order as defined by Rider Waite.
- The player who draws The Fool in this step must place The Fool on bottom and redraw.
- The player with the lowest value card plays the first trick in the given round.

### Tarot Gameplay Rules:
We use the following rules for our one round of Tarot gameplay (see links posted above for rules referenced):

#### The Deal
0. Tarot Game is a three to five player game, no more, no less.
1. Reorder all cards (1 - 78).
2. Separate Major Arcana from Minor Arcana.
3. Shuffle all Minor Arcana together, 1st half in right hand reversed, 2nd half upright, both sides face down.
4. Shuffle all Major Arcana together, 1st half in right hand reversed, 2nd hand upright, both sides face down.
5. Shuffle Minor Arcana and Major Arcana together, both sides face down.
- This shuffling ensures a good distribution between Major and Minor Arcana when dealing packets to players, in the hopes of reducing the chances for Annulment, as described below.
6. Depending on the number of players in the game, deal out cards to the dog and each player until all cards are dealt.
- Always deal cards vertical to each player. Vertical alignment matters less for gameplay, but more so for driving narrative during each play.
- Packets must be dealt in full to each player, and cannot be broken up in any way.
- For four to five players, packet size is always three, meaning the dealer deals three consecutive cards to each player to form the packet. For three players packet size is four cards.
- For three to four players, dog size is always six, meaning six individual cards must be dealt to the dog during the deal. For five players dog size is three cards.
- The dog cannot be dealt the first card or the last card of the shuffled deck.
- The dog cannot be dealt two consecutive cards.
- Deal sequencing is randomized following the above rules, with no valid permutation repeated consecutively for the entire deal.
7. Each player looks at their dealt cards and checks for Annulment.
- If any player has only one Major Arcana in their hand that is NOT The Fool AND that hand is also the smallest valued hand, reshuffle the cards and deal again (start from Step 1 above).
- Smallest valued hand is determinable by having the most low value cards, such as having all twos, threes and fours in all suits, etc.
- Repeat these steps until no player declares Annulment.

#### The Bid
8. Each player examines the value of their dealt cards to perform a bid, starting with first player and moving counterclockwise.
- If a player does not want to bid, they may pass to the next player and are removed from the bid.
- When a player makes a bid, consecutive players may only make a bid that is higher in value than the previously made bid, or they may pass the bid to the next player.
9. The winner of the bid becomes the Taker, and attempts to complete their bid contract in the round.
- The other players become Defenders and attempt to prevent the Taker from completing their bid contract.
- The bid types are as follows:
- -> "Small": if this is the winning bid the player adds the dog to their hand and puts six cards from their hand into their score pile face down.
- -> "Guard": the same as "Small" except the winner reveals the cards in the dog before adding them to their hand.
- -> "Guard without Dog": The dog is added to the bid winner's score pile face down.
- -> "Guard against Dog": The dog is added to the defenders' score pile face down.
- Players may never set aside King cards or Major Arcana after winning the bid, unless they cannot discard anything else, in which case they reveal which ones they discard and leave the rest face down.
- Major Arcana 0, Major Arcana 1 and Major Arcana 21 may never be discarded to the score pile after winning the bid.

#### The Main Phase
10. The player who won first trick (had the lowest card value) plays the first trick of the game.
11. Play per trick happens as follows:
- The first player plays any card from their hand, either a Minor Arcana or a Major Arcana.
- Consecutive plays must be one of the following:
- -> Minor Arcana of the same suit and higher value if available, otherwise just match the suit.
- -> Major Arcana if no available Minor Arcana of the same suit.
- Once a player plays a Major Arcana, consecutive plays must be one of the following:
- -> Major Arcana that is higher in value if available, otherwise any Major Arcana.
- -> If no Major Arcana or matching Minor Arcana, then any card from the hand.
- Play continues counterclockwise until all players play one card.
12. The player with the highest value card wins this play, and subsequently takes all played cards and puts them in their associated score pile.
- The winner then plays the next first trick, with play continuing counterclockwise until all players play another trick.
13. This flow of play repeats until all cards have been played.

### Tarot Strategies:
The Taker has one of two decisions to make before game starts:
- They put their lowest face cards into their discard pile, OR, they put their lowest normal cards into their discard pile.
- Both decisions are significant strategy-wise and narrative-wise.
- The default choice made will be discarding the lowest normal cards to the discard pile.

## Narrative Structure
A Story can be broken down into basic building blocks:
1. "What is the" Inciting Incident?
2. "What is the" Complication?
3. "What is the" Crisis?
4. "What is the" Climax?
5. "What is the" Resolution?
- We call these building blocks the "Five Building Block Questions," denoted as FBBQ.

Each layer of Story MUST answer the FBBQ.
- We define the different layers as follows:
1. Beats (the smallest unit of Story)
2. Scenes (basic building blocks of Story)
3. Sequences / Chapters (minor life stages in Story)
4. Acts (major life stages in Story)
5. Subplots (controlling theme amplifiers, useful in long Story formats)
6. Global Story (what the author is trying to say)

We have created the following sentence templates using grammar rules and applying Tarot card orientations:
1. Inciting Incident = Subject (Player) + Upright Verb + Upright Direct Object (Noun)
2. Complication = Subject (Player) + Reversed Verb + Reversed Direct Object (Noun)
3. Crisis = Question Word (D6, What, Where, Why, Who, When, How) + Auxiliary Verb (D7, Be, Do, Can, May, Have, Will, Should) + Subject (Player) + Reversed Verb + ?
4. Climax = Subject (Player) + Upright Verb + Upright Direct Object (Noun)
5. Resolution = Subject (Player) + Upright Verb + Upright Direct Object (Noun) + Conjunctive (D8, For, And, But, Or, Yet, So, However, Therefore) + Subject (Player) + Reversed Verb + Reversed Direct Object (Noun)
- We roll a die to obtain grammar words randomly, providing us with varying interpretations.
- IF desired, these values can be changed to desired values AFTER Story generation is complete.

To summarize everything we have discussed:
- We can now relate the card interpretations directly to the narrative building blocks using grammar extrapolation AND answering the FBBQ.
- This is the connection we implement in our algorithm to have our game of French Tarot generate for us our Story collection.

## "Arcanum" Algorithm
### Definitions
- We begin by deciding on how many rounds of French Tarot we want to play. This also decides the size of our collection of Stories, with range 1 - 20.
- One round of French Tarot is one Story. Three rounds is three Stories (Trilogy), and six rounds is six Stories (Hexology).
- Tarot is a three to five player game, giving us three to five main Characters in our Story.
- The winner of the bid is the protagonist of the Story, with the other players as other main Characters within the Story.
- Each card played by a player represents "a Scene driven by that player."
- Each Scene answers the FBBQ.
- Each set of tricks of Tarot represents a sequence of player Scenes, which we call a Chapter.
- Each Chapter answers the FBBQ.
- The number of Chapters a Story has is based on the number of players in a game of French Tarot:
- -> for three players, there are 24 sets of tricks, giving us 24 Chapters.
- -> for four players, there are 18 sets of tricks, giving us 18 Chapters.
- -> for five players, there are 15 sets of tricks, giving us 15 Chapters.
- In each of these instances, we add Chapter 0 for the Prologue, accounting for who won the first trick of the given round.
- We assume that each Story involves three Acts, following the 25/50/25 novel distribution.
- Each Act answers the FBBQ.
- We assume that every Story involves an amount of Subplot equal to the following:
- -> Support Characters are represented by the available Major Arcana in the Tarot deck.
- -> If the first trick played within a set of tricks is a face card, the Chapter perspective goes to the associated Support Character defined by the value of the Major Arcana.
- -> The number of times when this occurs is the total amount of Subplot defined in our Story.
- Each defined Subplot answers the FBBQ.
- We finalize our Story using the Global Story.
- The Global Story answers the FBBQ.

### Algorithm Walk-Through
- We start with creating our Tarot Deck, by uploading a JSON containing each card and relevant data.
- We decide how many rounds of French Tarot we want to play, ranging from 1 to 20.
- We decide how many main Characters are defined in our story, ranging from 3 to 5.
- For each Character, we do a Body Reading, which builds our Character using Tarot card meanings.
- After each Character is developed, we initiate our game(s) of French Tarot.
- We play a round of French Tarot to obtain all the data we need per Story.
- -> If we are playing three rounds of French Tarot, then we are creating three Stories.
- -> For a game with three Characters, we will have 24 tricks plus deciding the first play, equalling 25 chapters.
- -> For a game with four Characters, we will have 18 tricks plus deciding the first play, equalling 19 chapters.
- -> For a game with five Characters, we will have 15 tricks plus deciding the first play, equalling 16 chapters.
- -> The Character who wins first deal in the Prologue plays the first trick of the game.
- -> In each Trick, the player who wins the current Trick wins the first play of the next Trick.

- After we play the game, we build our (as square as possible) Story matrix with size = number of tricks + 1, giving us the following matrices:
- Three Main Characters:  
  | 0 | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |  
  | 1 | 6 | 11 | 16 | 21 |  
  | 2 | 7 | 12 | 17 | 22 |  
  | 3 | 8 | 13 | 18 | 23 |  
  | 4 | 9 | 14 | 19 | 24 |  
- Four Main Characters:  
  | 0 | 5 | 10 | 15 |  
  | 1 | 6 | 11 | 16 |  
  | 2 | 7 | 12 | 17 |  
  | 3 | 8 | 13 | 18 |  
  | 4 | 9 | 14 |  X |  
- Five Main Characters:  
  | 0 | 4 |  8 | 12 |  
  | 1 | 5 |  9 | 13 |  
  | 2 | 6 | 10 | 14 |  
  | 3 | 7 | 11 | 15 |  
- We now define the Global Story by using the diagonal of the corresponding matrix, giving us concrete bounds on our narrative structure:
- -> 0 = Prologue = Global Story Inciting Incident
- -> (1 - 6) or (1 - 6) or (1 - 4) = Act 1 AND Global Story Complication
- -> (7 - 11) or (7 - 11) or (5 - 9) = Act 2 AND Global Story Crisis
- -> (12 - 17) or (12 - 17) or (10 - 14) = Act 2 AND Global Story Climax
- -> (18 - 24) or (18) or (15) = Act 3 AND Global Story Resolution
- Based on the Tarot Rounds, we need to determine "where and when" each Subplot occurs
- -> Refer to the "Trick is Face Card" definition given above
- -> The Round these events happen is where we define our Subplot in our Matrix
- -> Subplots CAN overlap with the diagonal of our Matrix
- With the Global Story and Subplots established, we fill in Act information between our Global Story concrete bounds:
- -> Three Main Character Structure: Ch. 0 = Prologue, Ch. 1 - 6 = Act 1, Ch. 7 - 18 = Act 2, Ch. 19 - 24 = Act 3
- -> Four Main Character Structure: 
- -> Five Main Character Structure: 
- With our (as square as possible) Matrix, we assign Act lengths following the 25/50/25 novel distribution
- -> We now answer the FBBQ within each Act, assigning each question to a Chapter
- -> The questions for each Act CANNOT overlap with the diagonal of our Matrix
- -> Following the 25/50/25 novel distribution, we need two Chapters per question in Act 2, which is twice that of Acts 1 and 3
- We are now ready to evaluate each Sequence/Chapter, which we equate to one Round of French Tarot
- For each Round, we answer the FBBQ based on turn order in the Round:
- -> Player 1 = The Trick = Inciting Incident + Complication
- -> Player 2 = The Defense (Part 1) = Crisis + Climax
- -> Player 3 = The Defense (Part 2) = Resolution
- -> Trick Winner = Controls the next Trick's Inciting Incident
- -> The above ties together each consecutive Sequence/Chapter through Resolution -> Inciting Incident, looping on our building block narrative structure
- We now evaluate each Scene within each Chapter, where Scene = Card played in Round
- -> Each Card must answer the FBBQ  from the perspective of the Character that played the Card
- We do NOT define Beats, as to give our output enough flexibility in finalizing the writing of our story

We have now answered all building block questions for all our defined layers of story
- We now generate our Rough Draft Output using our Matrix dataset, containing card interpretations and game round results
- We use our noun extrapolation rules defined above to answer each set of FBBQ at each level
- These questions are the sentences we use to fill in each level of our story
- After filling in each section, we return the resulting collection of sentences
